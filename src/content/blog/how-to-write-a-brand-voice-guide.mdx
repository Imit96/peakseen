Your brand voice is how your company sounds when it speaks. Not what it says — how it says it.

Without a documented voice guide, every person writing for your brand — your social media manager, your developer writing error messages, your sales rep composing a proposal — is making it up individually. The result is a brand that sounds different depending on who is in the room that day.

Here is a framework you can use right now to define and document your brand voice.

## Voice vs Tone: Get This Clear First

These two terms are often used interchangeably. They are not the same.

**Voice** is your brand's consistent personality. It does not change. You are either direct or you are not. You are either warm or you are not. Voice is character.

**Tone** is how that voice adapts to context. Your voice stays the same whether you are announcing an exciting product launch or responding to a customer complaint — but the tone shifts. A confident, warm brand uses a celebratory tone for launches and a more careful, empathetic tone for complaints.

Think of it like this: a person has one personality but adjusts their manner depending on whether they are in a job interview or at a friend's birthday. Same person. Different tone.

## Step 1: Define Three to Four Voice Attributes

A voice attribute is an adjective that describes a consistent quality of how your brand communicates. Three or four attributes is the right number — fewer than three and you have not defined anything; more than four and no one can hold them all in mind.

**How to find yours:**

- List the ten adjectives that describe your brand at its best
- Remove any that describe what you do rather than how you sound ("reliable", "affordable" describe value, not voice)
- Remove any that could apply to every company in your category ("professional", "innovative")
- Pick the three or four that are actually distinctive to you

**Examples of strong voice attributes:**
- Direct. Warm. Grounded.
- Sharp. Generous. Unhurried.
- Confident. Plain-speaking. Curious.

<Callout type="tip">
For each attribute you choose, write one sentence explaining what it means in practice for your brand specifically. "Direct" means different things to a legal firm and a streetwear brand. Specificity is what makes the guide usable.
</Callout>

## Step 2: Write Do / Don't Examples for Each Attribute

This is the step most brand voice guides skip, and it is the most important one.

An abstract attribute like "direct" is useless to a writer without concrete examples. Show, do not tell.

For each attribute, write:
- **What it sounds like in practice** (two or three example sentences)
- **What it does NOT sound like** (the failure mode)

**Example — Attribute: Direct**

Do: "You have a brand problem. Here is how to fix it."
Do: "This takes 10 minutes. Here is what you will walk away with."

Don't: "We are pleased to offer our comprehensive suite of brand strategy solutions designed to help businesses of all sizes navigate the complex landscape of modern brand communication."

**Example — Attribute: Warm**

Do: "We know this decision matters. We are not going to rush you."
Do: "Good question — here is the honest answer."

Don't: "Per our previous correspondence regarding your inquiry..."

Write examples across different contexts: social media, error messages, onboarding copy, sales emails, product descriptions. The same voice should be recognisable in all of them.

## Step 3: Define Your Tone Variations

Document at least three tone scenarios and how your voice adapts:

- **Celebratory** (product launch, customer win, milestone) — warmer, more energetic, permission to be playful
- **Educational** (blog post, onboarding, help content) — clear, patient, focused on making the reader capable
- **Crisis or complaint** (something went wrong) — steady, empathetic, specific about next steps — never defensive

For each scenario, write one example paragraph demonstrating the right tone.

## Step 4: Document What You Are Not

A voice guide should be explicit about the personality traits that are off-limits. This is as useful as defining what you are.

Common examples:
- We are not corporate. We do not use jargon to sound authoritative.
- We are not casual to the point of careless. Warmth does not mean typos.
- We are not preachy. We do not lecture customers about their choices.
- We are not self-deprecating. We believe in what we do.

## Step 5: Put It in a Document Your Team Will Actually Use

A 40-page PDF in a shared drive is not a voice guide — it is a document no one reads.

A usable voice guide:
- Fits on two to four pages (or a well-structured Notion page)
- Has real examples, not abstract principles
- Is versioned and dated
- Has a clear owner who updates it when the brand evolves
- Is linked in your onboarding doc so every new hire sees it on day one

Include a one-page quick reference that covers: the three or four attributes, one do and one don't per attribute, and the tone variation rules. That is what writers will pin to their wall.
