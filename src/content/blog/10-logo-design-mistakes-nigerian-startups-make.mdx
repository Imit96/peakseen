Nigerian startups are building world-class products. The branding does not always match.

This is not a criticism — it is a pattern. The same ten mistakes appear across fintech, agritech, logistics, health, and SaaS companies at the early stage. Most of them come down to speed, budget pressure, and a lack of clear brief. All of them are fixable.

Here are the ten mistakes, and what to do instead.

## 1. Designing for Pitch Deck, Not for Product

The logo looks great at 400px wide in a slide. It falls apart at 32px in a browser tab or as an app icon on a phone screen.

**Fix:** Test your logo at 16px, 32px, and 64px from day one. If it is not readable at those sizes, it is not finished.

## 2. Using Too Many Colours

Three, four, sometimes five colours in a single logo mark. Each colour adds production cost (printing, embroidery, signage) and reduces flexibility across different backgrounds.

**Fix:** One primary colour in your mark. Two at most. The rest belongs in your broader brand palette, not the logo.

## 3. Fonts Downloaded From a Free Site

Fontspace and Dafont are not font foundries. The licences are often unclear, the quality is inconsistent, and you will find the same typeface on a competitor's packaging within twelve months.

**Fix:** Budget for a licensed typeface. Google Fonts has excellent free options with proper open-source licences — Inter, Plus Jakarta Sans, and Bricolage Grotesque are all usable for a professional wordmark.

## 4. The Globe, The Rocket, The Handshake

Every pan-African platform seems to have a globe. Every delivery startup has a rocket. Every B2B company has interlocked shapes implying partnership.

**Fix:** Ask what makes your business specifically different — not what category it is in. That difference is where your mark should come from.

## 5. Copying a Foreign Brand's Visual Language

There is a Lagos fintech that borrows heavily from Revolut. There is a Nairobi logistics company that is clearly inspired by DHL's angle system. The problem is not admiration — it is that these marks telegraph "local imitation" to any investor or customer who has seen the original.

**Fix:** Use international brands as a benchmark for quality and execution, not for creative direction. Your reference points for aesthetics should be broader than your direct industry.

<Callout type="tip">
When building your brief, collect visual references from outside your industry — architecture, fashion, packaging — and describe the feeling you want, not the logos you like.
</Callout>

## 6. No Monochrome Version

The logo was designed in full colour and only exists in full colour. Then comes a black-and-white print requirement, a single-colour emboss, or a watermark use case — and the mark does not hold up.

**Fix:** Every logo needs a full-colour version, a single-colour dark version, and a single-colour light (reversed) version. These are not optional extras.

## 7. Typography and Mark at Unequal Visual Weight

The icon is bold and heavy. The wordmark is a thin, light-weight typeface. They do not feel like they belong together, and at small sizes the wordmark becomes illegible while the mark dominates.

**Fix:** The visual weight between your mark and your wordmark should be calibrated, not left to accident. If your mark is geometric and bold, your wordmark needs substance to match.

## 8. The Brief Was "Make It Look Professional"

This is the most common brief PeakSeen receives from founders who have had bad design experiences. "Professional" is not a direction — it is a standard. Without specific guidance on personality, positioning, and audience, the designer defaults to safe, generic, and forgettable.

**Fix:** A useful brief answers: Who is this for? What do we want them to feel when they see it? What must it never look like? What three words describe us?

## 9. Freelancer Delivers PNG, Nothing Else

The job is done. You have a PNG. No source file, no vector, no colour codes, no font names. Six months later you need to resize it for a billboard or export it for a developer and you have nothing to work with.

**Fix:** The deliverables for a logo project must include: SVG and AI/EPS source files, PNG at multiple sizes, documented hex codes and font names, and a basic usage guide. If a freelancer will not provide these, do not hire them.

## 10. Rebranding Every 18 Months

The logo is changed whenever the founders get tired of it, the product pivots slightly, or a new designer joins and finds the old mark embarrassing. Frequent rebrands destroy brand equity before you have built any.

**Fix:** Build the brand properly once. Evolve the identity gradually as the company matures rather than resetting it. Mark version history and be disciplined about what triggers a rebrand versus a brand refresh.
