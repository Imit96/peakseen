A scroll-stopping social profile is not about posting beautiful photos. It is about being instantly recognisable. When someone lands on your profile, within three seconds they should know exactly who you are, what you do, and whether it is for them. That recognition is built through consistency — not creativity.

Here is how to build it from scratch.

---

## Step 1: Lock Down Your Core Colour Palette

Pick **three to five colours** and use nothing else. This single decision does more for visual consistency than any other choice.

- **One primary colour** — your dominant brand colour, used most often
- **One secondary colour** — used for accents and contrast
- **One to two neutral colours** — backgrounds, text overlays
- **One optional pop colour** — for CTAs or highlights, used sparingly

Write down the exact hex codes. Save them somewhere you will actually use — in Canva as a brand kit, in your phone notes, wherever you work.

<Callout type="tip">
Do not choose your colours based on what you like. Choose them based on what your audience expects to see and what sets you apart from your direct competitors. Scroll your competitors' profiles before you decide.
</Callout>

---

## Step 2: Choose Two Fonts and Stop There

Typography is the second fastest way to look inconsistent. Most business profiles use five different fonts across their posts without realising it.

Pick one font for **headlines** and one for **body text or captions**. Use them everywhere, every time.

- Free options: Google Fonts (Canva has these built in)
- Pair a display or serif font for headlines with a clean sans-serif for body text
- Never use the platform's default font for designed posts

---

## Step 3: Define Your Content Pillars

Your aesthetic is not just visual — it is structural. **Content pillars** are the three to five topics you post about consistently. They shape what your feed looks like because they shape what you photograph, what you design, and what you write.

Examples for a brand studio:

- **Expertise** — tips, frameworks, industry insights
- **Work** — case studies, before/after, behind the scenes
- **Culture** — team, values, how you work
- **Proof** — testimonials, results, client wins
- **Education** — breakdowns of concepts your audience needs to understand

When you have defined pillars, you stop staring at a blank caption wondering what to post. You already know.

---

## Step 4: Build a Template System

Designing every post from scratch is how you end up with an inconsistent feed. Build a small set of templates and reuse them.

**Minimum viable template set:**

- One **quote or tip** template — strong text on brand background
- One **carousel cover** template — clear heading, consistent layout
- One **image with text overlay** template — for photos or mockups
- One **promotional** template — for offers, announcements, launches

You do not need 20 templates. You need four good ones you will actually use. Build them in Canva, Figma, or Adobe Express. Lock the colours and fonts so nothing drifts.

<Callout type="tip">
Name your templates clearly and keep them in one shared folder. The harder templates are to find, the less you will use them — and the more you will improvise your way into inconsistency.
</Callout>

---

## Step 5: Standardise Your Photography Style

If you use real photos — of your product, your workspace, your team — they need to match. Inconsistent photography kills an otherwise solid visual system.

Decide on:

- **Light or dark tones** — bright and airy, or moody and dark?
- **Warm or cool** — which direction do your images lean?
- **Props and setting** — what objects, surfaces, and environments appear consistently?
- **Editing preset** — apply the same Lightroom or VSCO preset to every image

You do not need a professional photographer for every post. You need a consistent style. A single well-applied preset can unify photos taken across weeks and locations.

---

## Step 6: Audit Before You Post

Before you schedule anything, do a grid audit. Open your profile (or use a grid preview tool like Planoly or Later) and look at nine to twelve posts together. Ask:

- Do the colours feel cohesive?
- Do the fonts match?
- Does the mix of content types feel intentional or random?
- Would a first-time visitor understand what this account is about?

If the answer to any of these is no, fix it before publishing. First impressions on social are made in a grid view — not a single post.

---

## The Tools That Make This Manageable

- **Canva Pro** — brand kit, templates, scheduling
- **Later or Planoly** — grid preview and scheduling
- **Adobe Lightroom Mobile** — photo editing presets (free)
- **Coolors.co** — colour palette generator and hex code storage
- **Notion or Google Sheets** — content calendar and pillar tracking

You do not need expensive tools. You need a consistent system with the tools you will actually use.
